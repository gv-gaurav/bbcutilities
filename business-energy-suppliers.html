Cheapest Business Energy Suppliers in the UK

The cheapest business energy supplier is not always the same for every business. Your price depends on where your premises are, how much energy you use, your meter type, and the length of contract you are willing to commit to. Two businesses on the same street can receive very different quotes.

Price is often the first thing businesses look at when reviewing their energy. That is completely understandable. Energy is one of the biggest ongoing costs for any UK business, and even a small saving per unit adds up to a significant amount over a full year.

What Affects the Price You Are Quoted?

Before getting a quote, it helps to understand what drives your business energy price up or down. The main factors are:

  • Your energy usage (kWh per year): businesses that use more energy often qualify for lower unit rates.
  • Your meter type: half-hourly meters used by larger businesses can access different tariff types that may work out cheaper.
  • Your location: energy prices vary across different regions of the UK due to network charges.
  • Contract length: committing to a longer fixed-rate deal often secures a lower unit rate.
  • Your current supplier: businesses rolling over with the same supplier year after year often end up on significantly higher rates than new customers.

Which Type of Energy Contract Does Your Business Need?

When you look at business energy deals, you will be asked to pick a contract type. There are two main options. Here is what each one means in plain terms.

Fixed-Rate Contract - Your Price Stays the Same

With a fixed-rate contract, you agree on a price and it does not change for the length of the deal - usually 1 to 3 years. Even if energy prices rise in the market, your bill stays the same.

This is the most popular choice for UK businesses. It makes budgeting easy because you always know what you will pay.

Flexible Contract - Your Price Can Go Up or Down

With a flexible contract, your energy price moves with the market. If energy prices drop, you pay less. If they rise, you pay more.

This type of contract also lets you leave your supplier more easily, without large exit fees.
